I wrote a Coindesk op-ed about decentralized cloud a month ago. In it, I expressed tentative optimism about where the industry could go if it got serious, and why we (the "decentralized cloud people") could potentially overtake AWS in the very long term. 

I'm no longer feeling so excited about the expected outcome of the industry. At least, I'm not excited about the potential of the players I currently see who are "in web3", and I'm not feeling good about the go-to-market strategies I'm seeing. I'll clarify this later. I'll end in cautious optimism, but there's a lot of ugliness to get through first.
### Vesting, "trying to get in early"

One of the core problems here is the tension between the difficulty of the things we're trying to do and the perverse timelines we've set for ourselves. These perverse timelines come from our unfortunate fate of having oriented our entire industry around the token. It's not just that tokens allow grifters to hype and extract capital for the low, low cost of technooptimistic PR, although that's certainly the default case. To build most things that accrue immense and stable value and return their investment, it takes years of hard work. 

On the other hand, we have tokens: the second the network is live, the token is by necessity liquid. Perhaps there are some vesting restrictions, but standard in web3 is 24 months. Extending this vesting schedule probably won't happen, because a vesting schedule is a mexican standoff between the investor and the founding team, and they both benefit financially by bringing their exit to USD closer to the present. For this to go otherwise, both parties would have to make an altruistic and self-sacrificing agreement for the health of the networks they build.

### i am not bullish on decentralized cloud. I am excited about the possibility of edge-based, local-first architectures to improve privacy and user autonomy, however, we have to be considerate of uptime concerns for UX and how difficult it is for individuals to keep hardware live- local-first is good if you assume a spotty device connection and a server that mostly respects privacy (like mastodon).

this is not ready for enterprise at all. these architectures are probably ready for defense and individual prosumer. more R&D needs to happen, and it cannot be rushed or shackled by this goofy industry.

new value accrual models will have to come along, because the current ones are wholly unsuited to developing the thing we want to exist.

personally, banyan's going after defense and prosumer with more local-first, privacy-respecting applications. we are no longer deluded about "decentralization", "privacy", "local-first", "content-addressing", or any other techno-libertarian pseudo-intellectual utopianism. we know we will need to bring real value to real people in order to attract them. this means easing their day to day workflows, starting with the UX and how it fits into their daily lives. We're re-architecting our product to fit into human lives and workflows, studying human-centered design and creative human-computer interaction, and starting conversations with researchers at the frontiers of these fields. We're still working with privacy-respecting data workflows, but instead of building something that clumsily stores data in a "decentralized and private" manner (a cool hackathon toy), we're building from first principles to put out burning fires in how they interact with data.
